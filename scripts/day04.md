## Part 1. Personal Questions (00:00 - 04:00)

**Q1. Tell me about a decision you regret and what you learned from it.**
> A decision I somewhat regret was accepting a project without fully clarifying the scope beforehand. Midway through, it became clear that the client's expectations were far broader than what we had originally agreed upon, which put considerable strain on my team. In hindsight, I should have insisted on a detailed written agreement from the outset.

**Q2. How would your colleagues describe you?**
> I'd like to think my colleagues would describe me as dependable and detail-oriented, though I imagine some might also say I can be a bit of a perfectionist at times. I tend to double-check my work thoroughly before submitting it, which occasionally slows me down, but it also means errors rarely slip through.

**Q3. What motivates you to perform well at work?**
> What genuinely motivates me is a sense of ownership over my work. When I feel that a project is truly mine, rather than just another task assigned to me, I naturally put in more effort to ensure it turns out well.

## Part 2. Opinion Questions (04:00 - 08:00)

**Q1. Do you think remote work will remain popular even after the pandemic has fully ended?**
> I'd argue that remote work is here to stay, albeit in a modified form. Granted, many companies have already called employees back to the office, citing concerns over collaboration. Nevertheless, the flexibility that remote work offers has proven too valuable for many employees to give up entirely.

**Q2. Should governments regulate the use of artificial intelligence more strictly?**
> To a certain extent, yes. While excessive regulation could stifle innovation, a complete lack of oversight poses risks that are arguably too significant to ignore, particularly concerning data privacy and job displacement.

**Q3. Is competition or cooperation more beneficial in the workplace?**
> Both have their merits, but I'd lean toward cooperation being the more sustainable driver of long-term success. Healthy competition can certainly push individuals to perform at their best, but when taken too far, it risks undermining trust among colleagues.
