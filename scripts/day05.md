## Part 1. Personal Questions (00:00 - 04:00)

**Q1. What's a professional achievement you're particularly proud of, and why?**
> One achievement I'm particularly proud of was leading a cross-functional initiative that reduced our team's average project turnaround time by nearly 20 percent. What made it especially rewarding wasn't just the measurable outcome, but the fact that I had to convince several stakeholders—each with competing priorities—to buy into a fairly unconventional workflow change.

**Q2. How do you typically approach building relationships with new colleagues?**
> I tend to take a fairly organic approach rather than forcing rapport. In the early days, I make a point of asking genuine questions about a person's background and role, partly out of curiosity and partly because it helps me understand how best to collaborate with them down the line.

**Q3. If you could change one thing about your current workplace, what would it be?**
> If I had to pick one thing, it would probably be the way feedback is delivered within my team. Right now, most feedback tends to be reserved for formal review periods, which means smaller issues often go unaddressed until they've had time to snowball.

## Part 2. Opinion Questions (04:00 - 08:00)

**Q1. Some argue that globalization has done more harm than good. What's your take?**
> That's a fairly contentious claim, and I'd push back on it, at least in its strongest form. It's true that globalization has contributed to job displacement in certain industries. That said, it's hard to overlook the fact that it has also lifted hundreds of millions of people out of poverty globally. On balance, I'd say the benefits have outweighed the drawbacks.

**Q2. Do you believe automation poses a net positive or negative impact on society?**
> I'm inclined to view it as a net positive, though I wouldn't dismiss the legitimate concerns people raise. Historically, every wave of automation has eliminated certain jobs while simultaneously creating new categories of work that didn't previously exist. The real issue, in my opinion, isn't automation itself but rather how well societies manage the transition.

**Q3. Is it better for a leader to be liked or respected by their team?**
> Ideally, a leader would be both, but if forced to choose, I'd lean toward respect. Being liked can sometimes tempt leaders into avoiding difficult conversations, which ultimately does the team a disservice. Respect, by contrast, tends to be earned through consistency, fairness, and competence.
